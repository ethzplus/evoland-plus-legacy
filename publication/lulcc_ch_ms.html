Introduction

The mountainous central European country of Switzerland, like the majority of countries globally, exhibits declining biodiversity due to multiple drivers such as urbanization, climatic change and environmentally deleterious agricultural practices. To address biodiversity loss, in 2012, the federal government of Switzerland ratified the Swiss Biodiversity Strategy (SBS). One of the goals of the SBS is the development of Switzerland’s Ecological Infrastructure (EI) as a “network of protected and connected areas that contribute to safeguarding the essential services of ecosystems for society and economy … and form the basis for a rich and resilient biodiversity” . Achieving this goal will require consideration of not just how Switzerland’s EI should be planned under existing conditions but how it’s development in space and time could proceed in concurrence with changes in the boundary conditions of the socio-ecological system within which it is situated.

In this regard, (Mayer et al., 2023) defined 5 narrative scenarios to frame the development of EI in Switzerland between 2020 and 2060 alongside expected demographic, economic and climatic changes in Switzerland. The scenarios were produced through a participatory process with the aim of envisioning nature-positive outcomes using the IPBES Nature futures framework. As such the scenarios can characterized as both exploratory and normative, encapsulating the development of a “functioning” EI from different perspectives of societal-nature relationships (ibid).

However, whilst descriptive scenarios are a useful starting point for planners and decision-makers, their legitimacy can be increased by operationalising the narratives through computer-based simulation modelling so that their spatio-temporal impacts on critical aspects of the study system can be visualized and explored in detail (Verburg et al., 2004). Such operationalization of scenarios is especially desirable when their realization would likely result in trade-offs that must be negotiated by society. This is pertinent in the case of EI, which, under the definition employed by the SBS, aims to balance both ecosystem service (ES) provision and biodiversity protection, which have often been acknowledged as discordant objectives.

In terms of operationalizing the specific scenarios of EI development from (Mayer et al., 2023), the proximal linkages between EI as a ‘network of areas’ at the landscape level in Switzerland and it’s intended outcomes for biodiversity and ES are difficult to model directly. Instead, it is logical to focus on a higher level of abstraction and model an intermediary, or distal, interaction between the physical expression of EI and these outcomes. A common component in the modelling of both measures of biodiversity (e.g species distribution) and ES provision (Gomes et al., 2021; Newbold et al., 2015) is land use and land cover (LULC). Furthermore LULC change (LULCC) trends are also a central theme in the SBS concept of EI as well as the scenario narrative’s of (Mayer et al., 2023). In particular, the role of protected areas and Other Effective Conservation Measures (OECMs) as components of the EI as well as other EI-relevant LULCC trends such as urban sprawl and the spatial distribution of agricultural production. On this basis, the goal of this research is to operationalize the scenario’s of EI development produced for Switzerland by (Mayer et al., 2023) by simulating their implications for trends in LULCC in Switzerland between 2020 and 2060 using a temporally dynamic Constrained Cellular Automata (CCA) model.

CCA models have enjoyed enduring popularity for the modelling of scenarios of LULCC for the last three decades (Aburas et al., 2016; Triantakonstantis and Mountrakis, 2012). LULCC-CCAs treat landscapes as a pixelated grid of cells of individual LULC states with cells changing state over time typically based on two processes. Firstly, the spatial distribution of potential LULCC is determined through calculation of cellular probabilities of change, often referred to as transition potential (TP), to alternate LULC states. The method by which this is achieved, differs between popular LULCC-CAs although it is commonly based on the quantification of correlative relationships with explanatory variables such as biophysical, socio-economic and neighbourhood (focal LULC) predictors using a statistical model (García-Álvarez, 2018). Secondly, the amount of cellular LULC changes that occur in a given simulation time step is prescribed by values of transition rates extrapolated from historic LULC data, with an allocation algorithm selecting the required number of cells to change based upon their transition probabilities .

Considering the two processes described above LULCC-CCAs offers several opportunities, or mechanisms, to operationalize aspects of scenarios. Firstly, the data supplied in the form of dynamic variables used to predict cellular TP values can be specific to the different scenario’s being modelled (Liao et al., 2023). For example, in scenarios contrasting differential levels of climatic change, the data provided may come from exogenous climatic models of different Relative Concentration Pathways (Song et al., 2019). A second mechanism is to alter predicted cellular TP values according to proscribed criteria, this can be done directly: such as spatially designated land management policies, for example reducing the potential for transition to urban LULC states inside environmental protection areas(Clerici et al., 2019; Daneshi et al., 2021; Frank et al., 2023; Gharbia et al., 2016; Peng et al., 2021; Poelmans and Van Rompaey, 2010; Price et al., 2017). Or indirectly by altering the influence of variables within the model used to calculate TPs(Gago-Silva et al., 2017; Troupin and Carmel, 2016). Another means is to adjust parameters of the LULCC-CCA’s allocation algorithm, that may for example increase the creation of new patches of LULC class cover relative to the expansion of existing areas (Prieto-Amparán et al., 2019). Finally, the influence of different scenarios can be incorporated through specification of the transitions rates (Cheng et al., 2020; Kiziridis et al., 2023; Molinero-Parejo et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2021; Schirpke et al., 2020; Shrestha et al., 2018).

Methods

Model overview

To perform scenario-based simulation of future LULCC in Switzerland between 2020 and 2060 we created a constrained cellular automata (CCA) model combining the statistical programming software R (4.2.2)(R core team, 2022) with the environment modelling software Dinamica EGO (7.2.0)(Soares-Filho et al., 2009). In the interest of open science the CCA model along with the research data and results has been made available alongside this manuscript (URL)

Figure 1 highlights the primary processes taking place within the LULCC-CCA model, the details of each these processes are summarized in the subsequent sections although for brevity some material has been presented as appendices.

Land use land cover transition identification

The process of identifying LULC transitions, i.e. pixel changes from an initial specific LULC class to a different final LULC class, was based upon a preceding study by (Black et al., 2023). We utilized classified LULC datasets for four time periods from the Swiss area statistics : 1981-1985; 1992-1997; 2004-2009; 2013-2018 (Note that date range is the extent of the data-collection flying period, and in fact each product represents a single LULC map). The LULC data is originally presented as point data generated from aerial photography, however for the purpose of modelling all maps were converted to 100m resolution rasters. Given that transitions are identified between two time points having four historic LULC maps meant that we had three LULC transition periods, with which to calibrate our model, which will henceforth be referred to using the latter year of the flying period as: 1985-1997; 1997-2009; and 2009-2018 respectively. Following (Black et al., 2023) we aggregated the original 72 LULC classes in the data to 10 classes: Alpine pastures; Closed forest; Glacier; Grassland; Intensive agriculture; Open Forest; Shrubland; Permanent crops; Settlement/urban/amenities; Static (see 5 for a table of class aggregations).

Instances of LULC transitions were identified by overlaying the maps for each transition period and extracting pixels on the basis of their specific changes in LULC classes between their initial class (start map) and final class (end map). With 10 aggregated classes this gives the potential for 100 possible class-class transitions which is unfeasible for modelling. Thus, as per (Black et al., 2023), we filtered to viable LULC transitions for each period by first excluding illogical transitions (e.g from static to other classes) as well as applying a threshold to remove transitions that led to an a change in less than 0.5% of the total area of the initial class.

The instances of LULC transitions were then combined with instances of non-transitions (i.e. pixels with the same initial class but a different final class), coded as 1’s and 0’s respectively, to give seperate datasets for each transition. These transition datasets were first identified using the entire LULC map region (i.e the extent of Switzerland). However, to better reflect the potential for localized differences in LULCC processes the datasets were sub divided according to the six biogeographical regions of Switzerland (Gonseth et al., 2001). A table listing the transitions identified for the calibration periods is included as 6 .

Cellular automata parameter calibration

Dinamica EGO uses a combination of two algorithms for allocating cellular LULC transitions, Patcher and Expander, which represent the processes of the creation of new patches of LULC classes and the expansion of existing patches respectively(Soares-Filho et al., 2009). These algorithms require the calibration of 5 parameters for every transition: Mean patch size, Patch size variance, Patch isometry, and the relative % of transitions to be allocated by each algorithm (i.e % by patcher, % by expander). To calibrate these parameters we first used the historic LULC maps from the calibration periods to estimate parameter values using the R package SDMtools , which were then refined using brute force simulation. This involved applying a random permutation (from normal distribution with mean = 0 and sd = 0.05 effectively 5% bounding) to each transition parameter value for 100 repetitions to produce candidate sets of parameters values. These candidate sets were then tested by using them in the Dinamica EGO model to simulate LULCC between 2010 and 2020 (i.e covering the final calibration period) using a 5 year time step. The results of these simulations were used to identify the candidate set of parameter values that displayed the greatest value of the minimum similarity score produced by Dinamica EGO’s “fuzzy reciprocal similarity” map comparison method (Soares-Filho et al., 2013; Soares-Filho et al., 2009). This set of parameter values was used as the basis for all future simulations with some scenarios involving deliberate modifications of values (2.5.2).

Scenario operationalisation

Transition rates

The cellular transition allocation process in Dinamica EGO is constrained by input in the form of rates of transitions (the % of the initial class area to be converted) for each time step in the simulation. To establish transition rates for each of our 5 future scenario’s we iterated between quantitative estimation and expert elicitation.

Initially, transition rates were calculated for each of the calibration time periods before being extrapolated (linear models) over 5 year time steps between 2020 and 2060. These extrapolated transitions rates were dubbed the reference rates and were applied to the values of LULC class extents in the final calibration year (2018) to estimate reference class areas for 2060.

These reference class areas were used as a prompt in discussions with experts involved in the scenario creation process to code whether statements within the scenario’s were perceived as likely to lead to an increase or decrease in the areas of certain LULC classes relative to the reference areas. Experts were encouraged to focus only on the direct impacts of statements and not potential indirect impacts, i.e statements referencing change in agriculture practices were linked to agricultural LULC classes only and the potential for subsequent impacts on forestry were only considered in corresponding statements. Importantly, experts were able to allocate different magnitudes of impact on LULC classes due to individual statements. This was realized in a simplistic manner through the allocation of a desired number of either pluses and minuses, each representing an equally weighted percentage change to the class area, which were summed for each class across all statements to give overall percentage impacts of the scenario on class areas. This process was first employed for the BAU scenario with the percentage class impacts used to modify the reference 2060 class areas to give projected 2060 class areas. THis was then repeated for the remaining scenarios (EI-NAT, EI-CUL, EI-SOC and GR-EX) however their areas were derived relative to the BAU rather than the reference areas. This is an important distinction as the scenarios are intended to represent deliberate changes to the status-quo represented by the BAU narrative.

Finally, it was necessary to dis-aggregate the projected class areas for each scenario back to areas and then rates for each transition. Given that it is possible to either increase or decrease the rates of transitions that result in the expansion of LULC class area simultaneous to altering transitions that result in class area contraction, this process of dis-aggregation represented an optimization problem. Our method to address this was to perform a first pass over the LULC classes and allocate areas to relevant transitions with respect to the proportion of area expansion vs. contraction exhibited by the LULC class in the calibration periods. In the case that there were multiple transitions contributing to either class expansion or contraction then the values were ascribed based on the relative contributions of each transition to historic class change rates. This gave us an approximation of the transition areal changes for each scenario across all time steps with further manual changes made in order to reduce the discrepancy between the sum of these and the expert projected class areas for 2060.

Manipulation of transition potential and allocation parameters

From the scenarios details (Mayer et al., 2023) we extracted statements, grouped according to drivers, that pertain to spatialized differences in the locations LULCC processes . From these statements we derived 8 corresponding spatial intervention mechanisms which were applied across the scenarios to give a total of 19 interventions. The majority of the interventions functioned by directly alterating cellular transition probabilities during the simulation time. , however one intervention instead involved alteration of the Dinamica EGO allocation parameters.
